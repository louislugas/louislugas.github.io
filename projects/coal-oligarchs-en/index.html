The year is 2100. You're sitting alone, wet with sweat, in your stuffy living room. The air is hot and heavy. However, it's still better than going out of the house. It's a lot worse out there. Opening the front door is like opening a giant oven. Nusantara, Indonesia's capital on Kalimantan island where you live, has long been a "ghost town".

The island of Borneo, once known as the home to the "Earth's lungs", now looks like a snippet of hell. There are countless abandoned mines, with a few forests left. Animals have gone extinct. Only a handful of humans have survived. Since decades ago, people have started leaving for more liveable places. Some decided to stay, only to die from heatstroke.

You try to be grateful. At the very least, you were born and raised in Nusantara, not in Jakarta. The former capital city was completely submerged a few years ago. Global warming's effects, which were always played down by your grandparents, turned out to be so severe.

The Earth's surface temperature has increased by 4.4 degrees Celsius since the preindustrial era, in line with the worst scenario envisaged by scientists in 2021. Sea levels have risen by 2 meters, triggering extreme floods and displacing more than 40 million people across the globe. Oh, another thing: climate change has caused 83 million heat-related deaths over the past 80 years.

All that makes you wonder, "How did we get here?" You remember reading a news piece on an international treaty aimed at limiting global warming to 1.5 to 2 degrees Celsius. If you're not mistaken, the treaty was adopted by nearly 200 parties in Paris, France, at the end of 2015.

You're intrigued to surf the internet. You start hopscotching from one news site to another. You're curious to find out why Indonesia was so adamant about keeping its coal mining industry going despite the worsening climate crisis.
